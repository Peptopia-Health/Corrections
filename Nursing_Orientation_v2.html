2 Definition of Harassment↑
🚨 Base Definition
Harassment is unwelcome conduct that is based on race, color, religion, sex (including sexual orientation, gender identity, or pregnancy), national origin, older age (beginning at age 40), disability, or genetic information (including family medical history).
TriggerWhen Harassment Becomes Unlawful
⚠️ Quid Pro QuoEnduring the offensive conduct becomes a condition of continued employment.
⚠️ Hostile EnvironmentThe conduct is severe or pervasive enough to create a work environment that a reasonable person would consider intimidating, hostile, or abusive.
3 Retaliation & Legal Thresholds↑
Legal StandardPolicy Mandate
🛡️ RetaliationAnti-discrimination laws prohibit harassment against individuals in retaliation for filing a charge, testifying, or participating in an investigation/lawsuit, or for opposing discriminatory employment practices.
📏 ThresholdsPetty slights, annoyances, and isolated incidents (unless extremely serious) will not rise to the level of illegality.
4 Offensive Conduct & Circumstances↑
💡 The Scope of Harassment
Unlawful harassment may occur without economic injury to, or discharge of, the victim. Furthermore, the victim does not have to be the person directly harassed—it can be anyone affected by the offensive conduct.
CategoryExamples & Details
🗣️ Conduct TypesIncludes offensive jokes, slurs, epithets, name calling, physical assaults or threats, intimidation, ridicule, mockery, insults, offensive objects/pictures, and interference with work performance.
👥 The HarasserCan be the victim's supervisor, a supervisor in another area, an agent of the employer, a co-worker, or even a non-employee.
5 Examples of Harassing Conduct: Cyber↑
Digital VenueSpecific Violations
📱 Social Media & WebPosts, direct/private messages, and/or group pages on social media sites, websites, or blogs that make harassing or disparaging comments about another employee.
👁️ CyberstalkingCyberstalking another employee's online presence, obsessively following everything he or she does or posts online.
6 Physical Indicators of Abuse↑
Indicator CategoryObservable Signs
🩺 Physical SignsMedical and correctional staff must be observant during evaluations and screenings. Common physical indicators of sexual abuse include difficulty walking, sitting, or standing, unexplained injuries, STDs, and abdominal pain.
⚠️ Behavioral & Other SignsOther indicators can include sudden alcohol or substance abuse as a coping mechanism. Conversely, a planned pregnancy is not an indicator of abuse.
7 Sexual Harassment: Quid Pro Quo↑
ConceptDefinition
DefinitionLatin for "This for That". Occurs when a supervisor or person in authority requests sexual favors in exchange for tangible employment benefits (raises, promotions, continued employment).
Authority RequirementGenerally requires the harasser to have authority over the victim's employment status.
8 Sexual Harassment: Hostile Environment↑
ConceptDefinition
SpecificsUnwelcome sexual advances, requests for sexual favors, or other verbal/physical conduct of a sexual nature that interferes with work performance.
ExamplesSexual jokes, suggestive comments, touching, or displaying sexually explicit images.
9 Gender-Based Harassment↑
ConceptDefinition
Non-Sexual ConductHarassment doesn't have to be sexual in nature. It can be based on gender/sex, such as negative comments about women in general or men in general.
10 Who is Involved?↑
RoleDescription
The HarasserCan be a supervisor, co-worker, or even a non-employee (vendor, patient, or visitor).
The VictimDoes not have to be the person harassed directly; it can be anyone affected by the offensive conduct.
11 Harassment Based on Other Classes↑
ConceptDefinition
Protected GroupsRace, Color, National Origin, Religion, Age (40+), Disability, Genetic Information.
ThresholdBehavior must be unwelcome and severe/pervasive. Petty slights and annoyances generally do not reach the legal level of harassment.
12 Defining Workplace Incivility↑
IntensityBehavioral Scope
Low IntensityDefined as low-intensity deviant conduct with ambiguous intent to harm. It is often unclear whether the actor intended to harm the target or was simply being thoughtless or rude.
13 Uncivil Conduct & Bullying↑
ConceptDefinition
ScopeIncivility can be defined as anything from bullying to isolation or exclusion of an individual.
BehaviorsInterrupting others, originating rumors, ignoring/excluding colleagues, or taking credit for others' work.
14 Policy Nuances: Intent vs. Impact↑
Non-Acceptable ExplanationsDisciplinary Stance
⛔ "I was just kidding"“I was just kidding” and “I didn’t mean to…” are not acceptable explanations and do not excuse harassing conduct.
⚖️ DisciplineThese phrases will not stop disciplinary action from being taken against the person engaging in such conduct. Policy focus is on the impact on the victim and environment, not just the intent of the actor.
15 Workplace Violence Categories↑
CategoryDescription
Type I: CriminalPerpetrator has no legitimate relationship with the business (e.g., robbery).
Type II: CustomerPerpetrator is a customer/client/patient of the business (Most common in healthcare).
Type III: WorkerPerpetrator is a current or former employee.
Type IV: PersonalPerpetrator has a personal relationship with an employee (Domestic violence spillover).
16 Industry Statistics: Healthcare↑
Risk FactorIndustry Reality
🏥 Healthcare RiskNearly 75% of workplace assaults occur in a health care setting.
Time AwayHealthcare workers are four times more likely to require time away from work due to violence than workers in other industries.
17 Warning Signs of Potential Violence↑
Indicator TypeObservable Behaviors
⚠️ Behavioral TraitsPerpetrators often exhibit newly acquired negative traits, such as deteriorating appearance/hygiene, erratic behavior, or reclusiveness.
🚨 Threat IndicatorsDirect/veiled threats, intimidation, sudden mood swings, or obsession with weapons.
📉 PerformanceUnexplained absences, decreased productivity, or refusal to accept criticism.
18 Workplace Violence: Potential Risk Factors↑
CategorySpecific Risk Factors
🔫 Clinical/External
  • Weapons among patients, their families, or friends.
  • Care of acutely disturbed, violent individuals.
  • Presence of gang members, drug/alcohol abusers, distraught family members.
🧠 Patient System Issues
  • Increasing number of acute and chronically mentally ill patients being released from hospitals without follow-up care, who now have the right to refuse medicine and who can no longer be hospitalized involuntarily unless they pose a threat to themselves or others.
🏗️ Environmental/Training
  • Isolated work with patients during exams or treatment.
  • Lack of training in recognizing and managing escalating hostile and aggressive behavior.
  • Poorly lit parking areas.
19 Action & Reporting↑
ScenarioRequired Protocol
Immediate ThreatCall 911 or Facility Security immediately if there is an imminent threat of violence.
ReportingEmployees must report all incidents of harassment, incivility, or violence to their supervisor or Human Resources. Wexford Health prohibits retaliation against anyone who reports in good faith.
20 Employee Assistance Program (EAP)↑
ResourceApplication
🤝 EAP SupportThe Employee Assistance Program (EAP) is an appropriate resource for employees who are exhibiting incivility or struggling with personal issues that affect their workplace behavior.
21 PREA Screening↑
ProtocolApplication
🛡️ Screening ResultsThe results of the PREA (Prison Rape Elimination Act) screen are used to aid in determining housing assignments, program assignments, etc. by the correctional staff to ensure safety and prevent abuse.
🚷 Juvenile HousingPREA specifies that juvenile offenders may not be placed in adult housing units. Strict sight and sound separation is required to protect youthful inmates.
22 PREA Screening Timeframes↑
StandardRequirement
⏱️ Arrival ScreeningAccording to the standards, offenders are to be screened within 72 hours of arrival at the institution for their risk of being sexually abusive or abused.
23 PREA Disclosures & Referrals↑
Disclosure ScenarioRequired Protocol
📅 Historical AbuseIf during an intake screen you discover an inmate was a victim of sexual abuse which occurred in the past (e.g., 3 months ago), you must schedule for a mental health evaluation within 14 days. Acute measures like rape kits are not applicable for historical incidents.
⚠️ History of Abusive BehaviorIf an intake screen reveals that an inmate has a previous history of sexually abusive behavior (e.g., raping a neighbor), you must schedule an appointment with mental health within 14 days for evaluation.
24 Acts Prohibited by PREA↑
CategoryProhibited Acts
🛑 Physical Abuse & ContactIntentional touching of the inner thigh, buttocks, breast, or genitalia of another person, and any genital penetration.
🛑 Staff Harassment & VoyeurismDerogatory verbal comments of a sexual nature made by staff, repeated requests for unwelcome sexual favors, and staff members watching an inmate undress for a shower.
🛑 Inmate HarassmentOffensive verbal comments or gestures of a sexual nature made by an inmate toward another.
25 PREA Risk Potential Indicators↑
Indicator CategoryRisk Factors for Sexual Abuse
⚠️ Increased RiskFactors indicating an increased risk potential for sexual abuse (either being abusive or being abused) include: Developmental or intellectual disabilities, History of sexual offenses, Mental illness, and a History of being sexually abused.
26 PREA Scope Clarification↑
Behavior CategoryClassification Distinction
🗣️ Inmate-on-Inmate Verbal HarassmentOffensive verbal comments or gestures made by an inmate towards another inmate are generally classified as facility rule violations, rather than falling under the strict federal PREA definitions of sexual abuse or staff sexual harassment.
📝 Staff Verbal HarassmentIn contrast, derogatory verbal comments made by staff are strictly prohibited under PREA guidelines.
27 Inmate Education & Zero Tolerance↑
ProtocolRequirement
📢 Information DeliveryInformation regarding the zero tolerance policy and how to report incidents of sexual abuse or harassment must be given to the inmate during intake. This ensures they are immediately aware of their rights and the facility's strict prohibitions against sexual violence.
28 Frequency of Victimization↑
ConceptReality in Confinement
🔁 Repeated AbuseIt is a misconception that most people are only sexually abused once while incarcerated. In reality, victims of sexual abuse in confinement are often targeted repeatedly if there is no intervention, making immediate reporting and protective actions crucial.
29 Terminology: Intersex↑
ConceptDefinition
🧬 IntersexIndividuals with a chromosomal structure other than XX or XY are defined as 'intersex'. Understanding this terminology is important for appropriate medical, mental health, and housing considerations under PREA guidelines.
30 Mental Health & Vulnerability↑
Vulnerability FactorStatistical Reality
🧠 Mental Illness RiskInmates with mental illness are at least 8 times more likely to suffer sexual abuse while incarcerated compared to the general inmate population.
31 Mandatory Reporting & Consent↑
ProtocolRequirement
🚨 Mandatory ReportingStaff are mandatory reporters. You must immediately report any alleged sexual abuse that occurred inside a correctional facility to designated supervisors and officials.
❌ Consent RequirementYou do not need to obtain consent from the inmate before reporting the abuse. Reporting is mandatory regardless of the victim's preference.
32 Cross-Facility Reporting↑
ScenarioRequired Protocol
🏢 Allegations at Another FacilityIf you learn of alleged sexual abuse that occurred inside a different correctional facility, you must notify the warden or superintendent. The warden/superintendent is then mandated to notify the facility where the alleged abuse occurred within 72 hours.
33 Evidence Collection↑
ProtocolTimeframe Requirement
🧬 Physical EvidenceGenerally, physical evidence of sexual abuse (such as through a rape kit or SAFE exam) can be collected within 72 hours of the incident.
🛑 Preserving EvidenceIf the alleged abuse occurred within a time period allowing for evidence collection, request the victim not to take actions that could destroy evidence. This includes: brushing teeth, drinking, eating, urinating, moving bowels, smoking, or showering.
34 Nurse's Role in Alleged Assault↑
Role/ResponsibilityDescription
👩‍⚕️ Standard ProtocolThe role of the nurse in an alleged sexual assault situation includes: treating the victim with respect and dignity, determining the time frame of the alleged occurrence (to preserve evidence), notifying the medical director, and completely documenting the incident.
35 Psychological Indicators of Abuse↑
Indicator CategoryObservable Signs
🧠 Psychological SignsCommon psychological indicators of sexual abuse include depression or suicidal thoughts, difficulty concentrating, feelings of guilt, and engaging in risk-taking behaviors. A positive self-image is NOT a common psychological indicator of abuse.
36 Responses to Sexual Abuse↑
ConceptClinical Understanding
🧠 Gender SimilaritiesDespite common misconceptions, men and women generally have similar responses to sexual abuse. Both male and female victims experience comparable psychological and physiological trauma responses, including anxiety, depression, PTSD, and feelings of isolation or guilt.
37 Adolescent Indicators of Abuse↑
Indicator CategoryObservable Signs in Adolescents
🧑‍🤝‍🧑 Common ObservationsAdolescent and juvenile victims may exhibit distinct behavioral and physical changes. Common observations include wearing multiple layers of clothes (to hide their body), sudden sexual promiscuity or hypersexual behavior, and severe mood swings.
38 Medical Examinations↑
ProtocolRequirement
🏥 Qualified PractitionersMedical exams following sexual abuse must be performed by a SANE (Sexual Assault Nurse Examiner) or another qualified medical practitioner to ensure proper forensic evidence collection and trauma-informed care.
